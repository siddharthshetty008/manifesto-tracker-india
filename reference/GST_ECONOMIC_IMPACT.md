## 1. GDP Impact

### Predictions vs. Reality

| Metric | Value | Type | Source |
|--------|-------|------|--------|
| Predicted GDP boost from GST | +0.9% to +1.7% of GDP | PROJECTION | Renu Kohli, CSEP / World Bank policy paper |
| Predicted export boost | +3.2% to +6.3% | PROJECTION | Renu Kohli, CSEP / World Bank policy paper |
| Actual GDP growth FY2016-17 (pre-GST year) | 7.1% | MEASURED | World Bank / MoSPI |
| Actual GDP growth FY2017-18 (GST launch year) | 6.6% | MEASURED | World Bank / MoSPI |
| Short-term GDP disruption | -0.5 pp decline in growth year of launch | MEASURED | World Bank India Growth Story (WP 8599) |
| 1% GST revenue growth --> GDP growth | 0.56% GDP growth | MEASURED (regression) | Gokhale Institute study (OLS, Q2:2017-Q1:2021) |
| 2025 GST reforms projected addition | +20 to 30 basis points (0.2-0.3 pp) to FY26 GDP | PROJECTION | Economist consensus, 2025 |

### Key Finding
**Short-term negative, long-term positive.** GDP growth dipped from 7.1% to 6.6% in the year of implementation (confounded by demonetization). Econometric analysis shows 1% GST revenue growth associated with 0.56% GDP growth. The predicted 0.9-1.7% GDP level boost has NOT been conclusively isolated from other reform effects.

**Attribution caveat:** Demonetization (Nov 2016) coincided with GST preparation, making it impossible to separate the two disruption effects cleanly.

## 2. Tax-to-GDP Ratio

### Before vs. After

| Metric | Before GST | After GST | Delta | Type | Source |
|--------|-----------|-----------|-------|------|--------|
| Indirect tax/GDP ratio | 4.70% (FY2020*) | 5.6% (FY2022) | +0.90 pp | MEASURED | PIB / ClearTax |
| Gross GST/GDP | ~6.1% (pre-GST indirect taxes/GDP) | 6.1% (FY2023) | 0 pp (recovered to pre-GST level after 6 years) | MEASURED | Renu Kohli, CSEP |
| GST/GDP adjusted (excl. compensation cess) | ~6.1% (pre-GST baseline) | 5.7% (FY2023) | -0.4 pp (below pre-GST) | MEASURED | Renu Kohli, CSEP |
| Tax buoyancy (indirect taxes) | 0.72 (pre-GST) | 1.22 (post-GST avg) | +0.50 | MEASURED | PIB / CAG Report No. 5, 2022 |
| GST buoyancy FY2023-24 | -- | 1.21 | -- | MEASURED | Government data |
| GST buoyancy FY2024-25 | -- | 0.96 | -- | MEASURED | Government data |

*Note: FY2020 figure is the most commonly cited comparable pre-pandemic indirect tax ratio.

### Key Finding
**Tax buoyancy dramatically improved** (0.72 to 1.22), meaning tax revenue now grows faster relative to GDP. However, **absolute GST revenue/GDP only returned to pre-GST indirect-tax levels by FY2023-24 -- seven years after implementation.** When adjusted for compensation cess, revenues were BELOW pre-GST levels as late as FY2023.

## 3. Logistics and Trade Costs

### Before vs. After

| Metric | Before GST | After GST | Delta | Type | Source |
|--------|-----------|-----------|-------|------|--------|
| Logistics cost as % of GDP (commonly cited) | 13-14% | 7.97% (FY2023-24) | -5 to -6 pp | MIXED* | Various / DPIIT-NCAER study (2025) |
| Logistics cost as % of GDP (NCAER consistent methodology) | 8.35% (2017-18) | 7.97% (2023-24) | -0.38 pp | MEASURED | NCAER reports 2019, 2025 |
| Average truck distance per day | 225 km/day | 300-325 km/day | +75-100 km/day (+33-44%) | MEASURED | World Bank GST assessment |
| Truck turnaround (Dedicated Freight Corridor) | 15-16 days | 2-3 days | -80% | MEASURED | DPIIT / Freight corridor data |

*CRITICAL CAVEAT on the "13% to 8%" claim:*

The widely cited "13-14% of GDP" figure originated from Armstrong & Associates (2016) and was popularized by Minister Nitin Gadkari. NCAER's own 2019 study using rigorous methodology found logistics costs at 8.35% of GDP for FY2017-18 -- suggesting the 13-14% baseline was **never accurate**. The actual measured decline is from ~8.35% to 7.97%, a modest 0.38 pp reduction.

### Logistics Cost Breakdown (NCAER FY2023-24)
- Total logistics cost: Rs. 24.01 lakh crore (~$268 billion)
- Freight cost: 81.19% of total
- Transport cost per tonne-km: Road Rs. 3.78, Rail Rs. 1.96, Coastal Rs. 1.80, Air Rs. 72.00

### Key Finding
**The headline "13% down to 8%" figure is misleading.** Using consistent NCAER methodology, the real decline is 8.35% to 7.97%. However, the **truck transit improvement is real and significant**: +33-44% more distance per day, largely attributable to elimination of interstate checkposts under GST's e-way bill system.

## 4. Inflation Impact

### At Implementation (2017)

| Metric | Value | Type | Source |
|--------|-------|------|--------|
| CPI items exempt from GST | 50% of CPI basket | MEASURED | RBI (2017) |
| CPI spike at launch (July 2017) | Yes, brief uptick | MEASURED | CPI data / NIPFP studies |
| Duration of inflationary effect | Cooled down by early 2018 | MEASURED | NIPFP - Sudipta Jha |
| Net GST impact on headline CPI | No statistically significant impact | MEASURED (econometric) | NIPFP - Sudipta Jha (2022) |
| Contradicting finding (food prices) | Positive effect on headline inflation via food prices | MEASURED (intervention analysis) | NIPFP WP 405, 2024 |

### At GST 2.0 Rate Rationalization (2025)

| Metric | Value | Type | Source |
|--------|-------|------|--------|
| CPI inflation reduction from GST rate cuts | -25 bps (Sep-Nov 2025) | MEASURED | SBI Research |
| Full fiscal year CPI reduction | -35 bps for FY26 | PROJECTION | SBI Research |
| RBI revised inflation forecast (FY26) | Cut from 3.1% to 2.6% (partly due to GST cuts) | MEASURED (forecast revision) | RBI Governor Sanjay Malhotra |
| CPI inflation October 2025 | 0.25% (historic low) | MEASURED | MoSPI |
| Household cost reduction from GST 2.0 | Up to 13% | PROJECTION | IMF 2024 Article IV |

### Key Finding
**At initial implementation (2017): GST was broadly inflation-neutral** according to Jha's econometric study, though some NIPFP researchers found food-price inflation effects. **At 2025 rate rationalization: clearly deflationary**, reducing CPI by 25-35 bps. The October 2025 CPI of 0.25% is historic but attributable to multiple factors beyond GST alone (base effects, food prices).

## 5. Formalization / Tax Base

### Before vs. After

| Metric | Before GST | After GST | Delta | Type | Source |
|--------|-----------|-----------|-------|------|--------|
| Registered taxpayers | 66.5 lakh (6.65 million) -- July 2017 | 1.51 crore (15.1 million) -- 2025 | +127% (+8.45 million) | MEASURED | PIB / GST Network |
| New registrations (outside old tax net) in first month | -- | 6.6 lakh (660,000) -- Jun-Jul 2017 | +660,000 | MEASURED | PIB (PRID 1535747) |
| New GST registrations FY25 alone | -- | 25 lakh (2.5 million) | -- | MEASURED | GST Network data |
| Informal sector share of employment | ~45-50% | Still large (exact post-GST figure unavailable) | Unknown | ESTIMATE | Borgen Project / various |
| E-invoicing mandate | Not applicable | Mandatory for businesses > Rs. 5 crore turnover | -- | POLICY | GST Council |

### Key Finding
**Taxpayer base more than doubled** from 6.65 million to 15.1 million. However, the **informal sector remains very large** (~45-50% of employment). The 2025 government drive to formalize food vendors, small retailers, and cash-intensive sectors suggests formalization is still incomplete. Digital infrastructure (e-way bills, e-invoicing) is the mechanism driving formalization, not just the tax itself.

Bibek Debroy confirmed: "the average rate must be at least 17% to be revenue neutral, but actual is 11.4%."

## 7. Interstate Trade

### Before vs. After

| Metric | Before GST | After GST | Delta | Type | Source |
|--------|-----------|-----------|-------|------|--------|
| Number of indirect taxes | 17 separate taxes | 1 unified GST (with CGST/SGST/IGST) | -16 taxes eliminated | MEASURED | Ministry of I&B / GST Council |
| Interstate border checkposts | Extensive (every state border) | Eliminated | -100% | MEASURED | World Bank GST assessment |
| Border crossing time reduction (eastern India) | Multiple hours at Assam-WB, AP-Odisha borders | >3 hours reduction per crossing | -3+ hours | MEASURED | Barnwal & Dingel (U. Chicago) |
| Household expenditure impact | -- | +2% to +23% across districts (from market access improvement) | +2% to +23% | MEASURED (econometric) | Barnwal & Dingel (U. Chicago) |
| Projected interstate trade increase | -- | +30% to +40% | PROJECTION | World Bank (2017 opinion piece) |
| E-way bills generated (cumulative to June 2023) | 0 (system didn't exist) | 325 crore (3.25 billion) | -- | MEASURED | TaxScan / GST Network |
| E-way bills monthly (Dec 2022) | -- | 8.41 crore | -- | MEASURED | PIB |

### E-Way Bill Growth

| Year | E-Way Bills Generated (crore) | Source |
|------|------------------------------|--------|
| FY2018-19 | 55.78 | PIB / GST Network |
| FY2019-20 | 62.88 | PIB / GST Network |
| FY2020-21 | 61.68 | PIB / GST Network |
| FY2021-22 | 77.39 | PIB / GST Network |

### Key Finding
**Interstate trade barriers were substantially eliminated.** The Barnwal & Dingel study (University of Chicago) provides the strongest academic evidence: border crossing times fell by 3+ hours in eastern India, and the market-access improvement raised household expenditure by 2-23% across districts. The 30-40% projected trade increase (World Bank) has not been independently verified with actual trade flow data. E-way bill generation grew from 55.78 crore to 77.39 crore in 4 years, indicating growing formalized interstate goods movement.

| Metric | Before GST | After GST | Delta | Type | Source |
|--------|-----------|-----------|-------|------|--------|
| Number of tax returns per year | Varied by state/tax type | 37 per year per state of operation | -- | MEASURED | GST Act / Avalara |
| Time on GST activities (micro enterprises) | -- | 28.6 hours/month | -- | MEASURED | Economic Survey data |
| Time on GST activities (small enterprises) | -- | 21.4 hours/month | -- | MEASURED | Economic Survey data |
| Time on GST activities (medium enterprises) | -- | 15.7 hours/month | -- | MEASURED | Economic Survey data |
| Compliance cost as % of turnover (micro) | -- | 6-8% of turnover | -- | MEASURED | TaxScan / Economic Survey |
| GST registration threshold (goods) | Varied by state | Rs. 40 lakh (most states) | -- | POLICY | GST Council |
| GST registration threshold (services) | -- | Rs. 20 lakh | -- | POLICY | GST Council |
| Composition scheme threshold | -- | Up to Rs. 1.5 crore | -- | POLICY | GST Council |

### Key Finding
**Compliance burden is high, especially for micro and small enterprises.** 28.6 hours/month for micro enterprises and 6-8% of turnover in compliance costs is significant. The 37-returns-per-year requirement (per state) is onerous. However, the composition scheme and quarterly filing options provide some relief. The **pre-GST compliance cost comparison is the major data gap** -- no rigorous study has measured the BEFORE state to enable a proper delta analysis. Anecdotal evidence suggests the pre-GST system was also burdensome (multiple tax filings to different authorities), but no hard numbers exist for comparison.

## 12. Attribution Caveats

### What CAN be attributed to GST with high confidence:
1. Elimination of 17 taxes and interstate checkposts -- direct policy outcome
2. Taxpayer base expansion (6.65M to 15.1M) -- direct registration data
3. Truck transit improvement (+33-44%) -- directly tied to checkpost removal and e-way bill
4. Tax buoyancy improvement (0.72 to 1.22) -- consistent with unified tax base
5. C-efficiency improvement (0.44 to 0.54) -- standard VAT efficiency measure
6. E-way bill system creating digital audit trail -- direct policy outcome

### What CANNOT be cleanly attributed to GST:
1. **GDP growth effects** -- confounded by demonetization (Nov 2016), COVID-19 (2020-21), global trade cycles, other reforms
2. **Logistics cost reduction (8.35% to 7.97%)** -- also driven by PM GatiShakti, Dedicated Freight Corridors, highway expansion, Sagarmala
3. **Inflation outcomes** -- influenced by monetary policy, commodity prices, food supply dynamics
4. **Formalization beyond registration** -- informal economy remains very large; registration does not equal genuine formalization
5. **Interstate trade volumes** -- no clean measurement exists; e-way bill growth could reflect compliance, not new trade

### Major data gaps:
- **No pre-GST compliance cost study** exists for proper before/after comparison
- **No measured interstate trade flow data** before vs. after (only projections)
- **Actual informal economy shrinkage** not quantified with post-GST data
- **Revenue Neutral Rate analysis was ignored** in practice -- rates were set politically, not economically
